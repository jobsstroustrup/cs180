Overview & Approach

This page documents Project 1 — Images of the Russian Empire (CS280A track). We reconstruct color photographs from Prokudin‑Gorskii’s three‑panel glass plates by splitting the scan into B, G, R channels, aligning G and R onto B, and stacking them as RGB. I built both a single‑scale exhaustive aligner and a coarse‑to‑fine pyramid with configurable feature and score combinations. I also implemented automatic cropping, white balance, contrast stretching, and a gentle 3×3 color mixing (BGR→RGB‑ish).

Alignment (physics & math)

The three panels are monochrome exposures taken through blue, green, and red filters. Since each exposure is of the same scene with the camera fixed, the panels differ primarily by a 2‑D translation. We estimate translation by testing candidate shifts (dy, dx) and evaluating a similarity score on the interior overlap only (no wrap‑around). Two feature spaces are supported: raw intensity and gradient magnitude \(\|\nabla I\| = \sqrt{((I(x+1)-I(x-1))/2)^2 + ((I(y+1)-I(y-1))/2)^2}\).

  • NCC (ZNCC): maximize zero‑mean normalized cross‑correlation; robust to brightness scaling/offsets.
  • SSD: minimize sum of squared differences; sensitive to brightness changes unless used on gradients.
  • Weighted NCC on gradients: same as NCC but only the strongest mutual edges (top keep_top fraction) influence the score, via weights \(w=a\cdot b\).

Search is either exhaustive in a fixed window (for small images) or a pyramid: downsample by 0.5 to a small size, search there, upscale the displacement, and refine within ±r at each finer level.

Why gradients beat raw intensity for alignment

Different filters and emulsions mean the three panels have different photometric responses (tone curves, exposure). Matching raw intensities assumes identical photometry and fails near spectral edges (e.g., red clothing). Gradients, however, respond to boundaries (object edges) and are far more invariant to monotonic tone changes. Mathematically, under an affine intensity transformation \(I' \approx \alpha I + \beta\), gradient directions are preserved and NCC on zero‑meaned gradients largely cancels \(\alpha,\beta\).

Bells & Whistles (CS280A)

  • Auto‑crop: sum edge energy per row/column; trim outside until at most crop_frac of energy is removed; a small crop_guard pre‑trims scan borders.
  • White balance: Gray‑World (equalize channel means) or Shades‑of‑Gray (Minkowski p‑norm, p≈6) to bias toward highlights.
  • Contrast: per‑channel percentile stretch (1–99%) to fill [0,1] while ignoring outliers.
  • Color mixing: a mild 3×3 linear transform from [B,G,R] to RGB‑ish primaries; helps reduce historical filter casts.
Params (main runs): search=180, feature=grad, score=ncc, pyramid=ON, margin_frac=0.15, post: none (Sections 1–2) vs. WB=grayworld + contrast 1–99% + color‑mix + auto‑crop (Section 3).
